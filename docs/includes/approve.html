Approve probe

Figure 1 shows the overall percentage of “yes” responses for both groups combined in the two intonation types. As seen in the figure, “yes” was more frequent in declarative items overall (41.5%) than in sarcastic items (33.9%). The figure represents a total of 1195 trials for the declarative and 1200 for the sarcastic condition.

Figure 2 shows the overall percentage of “yes” responses for both groups individually in the two intonation types. “Yes” was the most frequent in declarative items for children (53.5%), followed by sarcastic items in children (43.7%). The adults had a lower percentage in both cases, but also had a higher percentage in declarative intonation(36.3%) than sarcastic (29.6%). Notably, there was more data overall in the adult data set than children: there were 1668 total data points for adults and 727 data points for children.

Figure 3 shows the overall percentage of “yes” responses for the of the three modifier types overall. “Yes” was the most frequent in grad+subj+pos items (72.4%), followed by -grad–subj items (28.1%), and finally grad+subj+neg with 12.5%).

Figure 4 breaks down the percentage of “yes” responses for the of the three modifier types by groups. The highest percentage was in children for grad+subj+pos (80.5%), followed by adults in the same condition (68.9%). In the –grad–subj modifier type, children again has a higher percentage of yes responses (44.7%) relative to adults (21%) The same trend was observed for the final modifier type of grad+subj+neg (children: 20.3%; adults 9%).

Figure 5 breaks down the percentage of “yes” responses for the of the three modifier types by intonation insead of groups. The highest percentage was for declaratives for grad+subj+pos (81.5%), followed by sarcastic items in the same condition (63.4%). In the –grad–subj modifier type, declarative utterances were shown to have a higher percentage of yes responses (31.8%) relative to sarcastic ones (24.4%) The opposite trend was observed for the final modifier type of grad+subj+neg, though the difference was small (delcarative: 11.2%; sarcastic 13.8%).

Model Results

Finally, Figure 6 breaks down the percentage of “yes” responses for the of the three modifier types by both intonation and between groups. Many of differences were tested directly in the Bayesian Logistic regression models. The logistic regression model are summarized in the tables below and test the differences the log-odds of answering “yes” for both sarcastic and declarative intonation in both groups. An individual model was run each of the three modifier types.

Table 1 shows the model for the modifier type -grad-subj and is visualized in the left panel of Figure 6. The intercept term predicts the log-odds of choosing “yes” for declarative items by adults -1.832 [95% HDI -2.45 - -1.298]. This corresponds to a median probability of 0.14 (ranging from 0.08 to 0.21). The fixed effect “intonationsarcastic” tests whether there was a difference in the adult group between declarative and sarcastic intonation. The model suggests that the effect is negative (-0.428), and possibly exists (pd = 0.953). This can be taken as moderate evidence that adults were less likely to respond “yes” to trials with sarcastic intonation than declarative intonation in the -grad-subj modifier condition, although the effect is small (predicted probability of .13 and .1 respectively. There was compelling evidence that the children were more likely to answer “yes” than adults in the declarative condition 2.009 [95% HDI 1.239 - 2.897; pd = 1]. Finally, there was not compelling evidence of an interaction -0.434 [95% HDI -1.21 - 0.38; pd = 0.849], suggesting that there was not reliable evidence that children were more likely to respond “yes” as a function of intonation for the -grad-subj modifier condition. However, there was also not sufficient evidence for practical equivalence, suggesting that this result is inconclusive, rather than null [ROPE bounds = +/- .18; ROPE percentage: 0.219].

Table 2 shows the model for the modifier type grad+subj+neg and is visualized in the middle panel of Figure 6. Again, the intercept term predicts the log-odds of choosing “yes” for declarative items by adults -4.282 [95% HDI -5.774 - -3.102]. This corresponds to a very small median probability of 0.01 (ranging from 0 to 0.04).

There was compelling evidence for an increase in “yes” responses in adults for sarcastic items relative to declarative ones [Median = 1.11; 95% HDI repmod2$CI_low[2] - repmod2$CI_high[2], and certainly exists (pd = 0.998). However, this effect is a rather small increase in which the model predicts the median probability to be just 0.04.

Like the first model, there was again compelling evidence that the children were more likely to answer “yes” than adults in the declarative condition 2.348 [95% HDI 1.302 - 3.545; pd = 1]. Unlike the first model, there was compelling evidence of an interaction -1.588 [95% HDI -2.785 - -0.472; pd = 0.998], suggesting that there children were overall less likely to respond “yes” when the intonation was sarcastic.
